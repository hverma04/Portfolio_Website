Assistant.

Ran Bloomberg Terminal open hours sessions, guiding students through historical data, economic estimates, and BQL — and observed the volatility patterns that would later drive a stock probability model and a quant research internship.

Role details

Open Hours Sessions

Teaching the Terminal

  • Led Bloomberg Terminal open hours sessions for students — walking through the platform function by function, from navigation to advanced data pulls.
  • Taught historical data retrieval: pulling time-series price and volume histories, dividend records, and earnings timelines across equities and fixed income.
  • Covered economic estimates workflows — accessing consensus forecasts for GDP, CPI, sector PMIs, and analyst estimate revision trends to contextualize macro positioning.
  • Developed student BQL (Bloomberg Query Language) proficiency: writing custom data queries, building equity screens, and automating bulk data exports for research workflows.
Model Building

Stock Probability Model

  • Observations from the trading room — specifically high-volatility growth in equities driven by incoming market trends and new information — inspired independent development of a stock probability model.
  • Model quantifies expected move distributions across market regimes, incorporating momentum signals, volatility clustering, and the asymmetric price impact of information events.
  • Built entirely from first principles; no external API dependency — the underlying thesis came from patterns watched live on the Terminal during sessions.
Quant Research Pathway

From open hours to research lab

  • The analytical depth built in this role directly supported pitching for a quantitative research internship at UMass Lowell — translating terminal fluency into a research credential.
  • Studied VIX volatility structure in the internship: term structure dynamics, implied vs. realized vol spreads, and the behavior of volatility during risk-off events.
  • Developed theories on how arbitrage profit can be extracted from volatility mispricing — targeting dislocations between implied volatility surfaces and realized distributions.
What This Role Built

Terminal fluency as a research foundation

Most people use Bloomberg as a lookup tool. Running open hours sessions forced a different relationship — one where understanding the why behind every function mattered, because students asked hard questions. That depth is what made the jump from terminal operator to probability model builder to quant researcher feel like a natural progression, not a leap.
